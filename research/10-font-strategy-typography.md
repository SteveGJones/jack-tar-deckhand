### Detailed Pairing Analysis

#### 1. Montserrat + Open Sans
- **Character**: Bold geometric headings over neutral, friendly body text. The contrast works because Open Sans has softer, more organic curves versus Montserrat's strict geometry.
- **Designer lineage**: Montserrat by Julieta Ulanovsky (2011, Buenos Aires inspiration); Open Sans by Steve Matteson (Ascender Corp)
- **Projection notes**: Both have large x-heights. Use Montserrat at SemiBold (600) or Bold (700) for headings. Open Sans Regular (400) is sufficient for body on projection due to its optimized open forms. Avoid Montserrat Thin/Light weights on projection entirely.
- **Weight hierarchy for slides**: Heading: Montserrat Bold (700) or ExtraBold (800) at 36-48pt; Subheading: Montserrat SemiBold (600) at 24-32pt; Body: Open Sans Regular (400) at 20-28pt; Caption: Open Sans Regular (400) at 16-18pt

Source: [Figma Montserrat Pairings](https://www.figma.com/google-fonts/montserrat-font-pairings/), [Maxibestof Montserrat + Open Sans](https://maxibestof.one/typefaces/montserrat/pairing/open-sans), [Wikipedia - Montserrat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montserrat_(typeface)) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 2. Playfair Display + Source Sans Pro
- **Character**: Refined serif headings with clean sans-serif body. The high-contrast serifs of Playfair command attention; Source Sans Pro's generous x-height ensures body readability.
- **Designer lineage**: Playfair by Claus Eggers Sorensen (inspired by 18th-century Baskerville); Source Sans by Paul Hunt for Adobe
- **Projection notes**: Playfair's thin hairline strokes can disappear on projection at smaller sizes. Use ONLY for large headings (36pt+). Source Sans Pro is excellent for body text at any projection size. The contrast between thick and thin strokes in Playfair makes it unsuitable for body text on slides.
- **Best for**: Elegant keynotes, literary events, luxury brand presentations, TED-style talks
- **Warning**: This pairing requires careful weight management. Playfair at Regular weight may lose thin strokes on projection; use Bold for safety.

Source: [Fontpair Playfair + Source Sans](https://www.fontpair.co/pairings/playfair-display-source-sans-pro), [FontFlow Playfair + Source Sans](https://fontflowapp.web.app/pairings/playfair-source) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 3. Poppins + Roboto
- **Character**: Geometric friendliness over studied neutrality. Poppins has rounder terminals and pronounced ink traps; Roboto is Google's workhorse with mechanical skeleton and open curves.
- **Designer lineage**: Poppins by Indian Type Foundry; Roboto by Christian Robertson for Google (2011, redesigned 2014)
- **Projection notes**: Both have generous x-heights. Poppins's strong vertical strokes maintain visibility. Roboto's 2014 redesign made it rounder and wider, improving display clarity. Both perform well at distance.
- **Weight hierarchy**: Heading: Poppins Bold (700) or SemiBold (600); Body: Roboto Regular (400) or Medium (500)

Source: [Fontdiff Roboto vs Poppins](https://fontdiff.com/compare/roboto-vs-poppins/), [Maxibestof Poppins + Roboto](https://maxibestof.one/typefaces/poppins/pairing/roboto), [Wikipedia Roboto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roboto) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 4. Inter (Single Family)
- **Character**: Swiss precision engineered for screens. The ultimate "it just works" choice.
- **Designer lineage**: Rasmus Andersson (2017). Over 2000 glyphs, 147 languages, variable font.
- **Projection notes**: Outstanding projection legibility. Tall x-height exceeds Helvetica's. Open apertures aid character distinction. Features ink traps and bridges specifically designed for challenging rendering conditions. The "text" optical size has enhanced contrast features; the "display" optical size offers clean lines.
- **Weight hierarchy**: Heading: Inter Bold (700) or ExtraBold (800); Subheading: Inter SemiBold (600); Body: Inter Regular (400) or Medium (500); Caption: Inter Regular (400)
- **Key advantage**: Single-family simplicity eliminates pairing risk. Variable font means a single file covers all weights.

Source: [Inter Font Family](https://rsms.me/inter/), [Wikipedia Inter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inter_(typeface)), [Etienne Aubert Bonn Inter Review](https://www.etienneaubertbonn.com/inter-font/) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 5. Raleway + Lato
- **Character**: Cool elegance meets warm approachability. Two humanist sans-serifs with distinct voices.
- **Designer lineage**: Raleway by Matt McInerney (originally), expanded by Pablo Impallari; Lato by Lukasz Dziedzic (2010, "Lato" = "Summer" in Polish)
- **Projection notes**: Raleway's lighter weights are particularly graceful but MUST be avoided on projection. Use SemiBold (600) minimum for headings. Lato's semi-rounded details create warmth and it performs well at body sizes. Lato was the 3rd most popular Google Font with 1B+ daily views.
- **Caution**: Raleway Thin/Light on projection will be illegible. This pairing requires disciplined weight selection.

Source: [Maxibestof Raleway + Lato](https://maxibestof.one/typefaces/raleway/pairing/lato), [LatoFonts.com](https://www.latofonts.com/), [Wikipedia Lato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lato_(typeface)) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 6. Nunito + Nunito Sans
- **Character**: Friendly and approachable. Nunito has rounded terminals; Nunito Sans has standard terminals for professional warmth.
- **Projection notes**: Uniform stroke widths work well for projection consistency. Neither font has stroke contrast issues. The rounded terminals of Nunito headings add visual friendliness without sacrificing legibility.
- **Weight hierarchy**: Heading: Nunito Bold (700) or ExtraBold (800); Body: Nunito Sans Regular (400) or Medium (500)

Source: [Google Fonts Nunito](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Nunito), [Google Fonts Nunito Sans](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Nunito+Sans), [BonFX Nunito Sans Pairings](https://bonfx.com/what-fonts-go-with-nunito-sans/) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 7. Work Sans + IBM Plex Sans
- **Character**: Industrial precision meets corporate clarity. Both are sans-serifs optimized for screen display.
- **Designer lineage**: Work Sans by Wei Huang (based on early Grotesques); IBM Plex by Mike Abbink with Bold Monday for IBM
- **Projection notes**: Work Sans core designed for 14px-48px on-screen use. IBM Plex has balanced x-height and open apertures. IBM Plex also has a Mono variant (Plex Mono) for code/data slides -- matching design DNA maintains consistency.
- **Best for**: Technical presentations, developer conferences, enterprise keynotes

Source: [Google Fonts Work Sans](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Work+Sans), [Beautiful Web Type IBM Plex Sans](https://www.beautifulwebtype.com/ibm-plex-sans/), [Typewolf IBM Plex Sans](https://www.typewolf.com/ibm-plex-sans) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 8. Oswald + Merriweather
- **Character**: Bold impact headings over comfortable reading serif body.
- **Projection notes**: Oswald is condensed -- this REDUCES distance legibility for body text but creates impactful headlines. Use Oswald ONLY for short headings, never body. Merriweather was specifically designed for screen reading with very large x-height and wide counters, making it an excellent projected body text serif.
- **Warning**: Oswald's condensed width means it works for short, punchy headings only. Any body text in Oswald will be difficult to read beyond the first few rows.

Source: [Fontdiff Merriweather vs Oswald](https://fontdiff.com/compare/merriweather-vs-oswald/), [Google Fonts Merriweather](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Merriweather), [Canva Font Pairing Guide](https://www.canva.com/learn/the-ultimate-guide-to-font-pairing/) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 9. Space Grotesk + DM Sans
- **Character**: Tech-forward, futuristic. Clean geometric lines.
- **Designer lineage**: Space Grotesk by Florian Karsten (2017, derived from Space Mono by Colophon Foundry); DM Sans by Colophon Foundry for Google (updated May 2023 with full variable font support)
- **Projection notes**: Similar x-heights create harmony. DM Sans's 2023 update added optical size axis improving rendering at different sizes. Space Grotesk lacks italics (only 5 weights, 300-700), which limits emphasis options on slides.
- **Limitation**: Space Grotesk has no italic variant. Use weight contrast instead of italics for emphasis.

Source: [Maxibestof Space Grotesk + DM Sans](https://maxibestof.one/typefaces/space-grotesk/pairing/dm-sans), [Google Fonts Space Grotesk](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Space%2BGrotesk), [FontFYI DM Sans Guide](https://fontfyi.com/blog/dm-sans-font-guide/) [Confidence: HIGH]

#### 10. Fira Sans + Source Serif Pro
- **Character**: Technical sans-serif headings over classical serif body. Mozilla heritage meets Adobe craftsmanship.
- **Designer lineage**: Fira Sans by Erik Spiekermann and Carrois Type Design for Mozilla (2013, adapted from Meta); Source Serif by Frank Grieshammer for Adobe (2014, inspired by Pierre Simon Fournier)
- **Projection notes**: Fira Sans was designed for legibility across varying screen quality -- explicitly optimized for projection scenarios. Source Serif's smooth curves and balanced weighting work well for extended reading. Fira's slightly wider proportion than Meta improves screen legibility.
- **Note**: Fira Sans development ended at v4.301 (2016); future development continues as FiraGO. For new projects, FiraGO may be preferred.

Source: [Wikipedia Fira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fira_(typeface)), [Mozilla Fira Blog](https://blog.mozilla.org/ux/2014/05/try-out-fira-sans-a-free-open-source-typeface-commissioned-by-mozilla/), [Wikipedia Source Serif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_Serif), [GitHub Source Serif](https://github.com/adobe-fonts/source-serif) [Confidence: HIGH]

### The 8H Rule

**Definition**: The distance from the screen to the last row of seats should be no more than 8 times the height of the screen. The smallest text should be at least 1/50th the height of the screen.

**Quick Formula**: Minimum text height on screen = Distance to Back Row (inches) / 400

**Example Calculations**:

| Room Length | Back Row Distance | Min Text Height on Screen | Notes |
|------------|-------------------|--------------------------|-------|
| 20 ft (240") | 240" | 0.6" | Small meeting room |
| 32 ft (384") | 384" | ~1.0" | Standard conference room |
| 60 ft (720") | 720" | 1.8" | Large auditorium |
| 100 ft (1200") | 1200" | 3.0" | Major keynote venue |

**Converting to PowerPoint Points**:
PTH = (PowerPoint Text Size x Screen Height) / PowerPoint Page Height (7.5" for 16:9)

**Important caveats**:
- The 8H rule provides MINIMUM legible sizes, not comfortable reading sizes
- Assumes normal vision; for older audiences, use the 6H rule instead
- If the room exceeds 8H distance, double the minimum text height
- These are minimums for footnotes/labels; body text should be substantially larger

Source: [Presentation Guild - 8H Rule](https://presentationguild.org/how-big-big-enough-the-8h-rule-reveals-all/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30 Rule

**The Rule**: 10 slides, 20 minutes, nothing smaller than 30pt.

**Rationale**: Using no font smaller than 30 points forces you to find the most salient points and explain them well. It prevents the audience from reading ahead and keeps them listening.

**The Age Algorithm**: If 30pt feels too dogmatic, find the age of the oldest person in your audience and divide by two. That is your minimum font size.

**Context**: Originally formulated for 1-hour venture capital pitch presentations. While widely adopted, it is a guideline rather than an absolute rule. For data-heavy technical presentations, strict 30pt minimum may be impractical -- but it remains an excellent default.

Source: [Guy Kawasaki - 10/20/30 Rule](https://guykawasaki.com/the_102030_rule/), [Six Minutes Analysis](http://sixminutes.dlugan.com/10-20-30-rule-guy-kawasaki-powerpoint/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Nancy Duarte's Recommendations

**Minimum Font Size**: Do not go smaller than 28pt for keynotes.

**The 3-Second Rule**: Your audience should grasp the main point of a slide in three seconds. This principle drives all typography decisions.

**Content Limits**:
- More than 75 words = you have created a document, not a slide
- More than 50 words = you have created a teleprompter

**The Monitor Test**: Measure your monitor diagonally in inches, display your slides, then step back the same number of feet. If text is legible at that distance, it will work on projection. (Example: 17" monitor -> step back 17 feet.)

**Sans-serif preference**: Sans-serif fonts may be easier to read on slides.

Source: [Duarte - Design Slides for Virtual Presentations](https://www.duarte.com/blog/design-slides-for-virtual-presentations/), [Duarte Slidedocs PDF](https://1619981.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/1619981/Duarte_Slidedocs_Ebook.pdf), [Medium - Slide:ology Summary](https://medium.com/@brijsethi/slide-ology-nancy-duarte-book-summary-835a3144a1c8) [Confidence: HIGH]

## Area 3: Font Embedding in PPTX

### How PptxGenJS Handles Fonts

PptxGenJS specifies fonts via the `fontFace` property on text objects and through presentation-level defaults (`headFontFace`, `bodyFontFace`). However, **PptxGenJS does not support font embedding**. The font name is stored as a string reference in the PPTX XML; the actual font file is NOT included in the output.

**Technical barrier**: PowerPoint uses proprietary `.fntdata` files containing rasterized glyphs rather than standard TrueType/OpenType font files. Reverse-engineering this format is impractical without Microsoft's internal specifications. A PR (#1302) for font embedding existed but remained unmerged as of early 2025.

**The pptx-embed-fonts package** (`npm install pptx-embed-fonts`, v0.0.6 by luxian) is a separate utility that can post-process PPTX files to add embedded fonts. It requires converting fonts from OTF/TTF to `.ms-fontobject` format. This could be used as a post-processing step after PptxGenJS generation.

Source: [PptxGenJS Issue #176 - Embedding Fonts](https://github.com/gitbrent/PptxGenJS/issues/176), [PptxGenJS Issue #1378](https://github.com/gitbrent/PptxGenJS/issues/1378), [PptxGenJS Text API](https://gitbrent.github.io/PptxGenJS/docs/api-text.html) [Confidence: HIGH]

### How python-pptx Handles Fonts

python-pptx similarly stores font names as string references in XML and **does not support font embedding**. There is an open GitHub issue (#355) requesting this feature with no implementation.

**Workaround**: Aspose.Slides for Python (.NET) supports font embedding programmatically, but it is a commercial library with licensing costs.

Source: [python-pptx Issue #355](https://github.com/scanny/python-pptx/issues/355), [Aspose Slides Embedded Font Docs](https://docs.aspose.com/slides/python-net/embedded-font/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Font Embedding Rules in PowerPoint

| Format | Embeddable? | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| TrueType (.ttf) | Yes | Standard embedding support |
| OpenType (.otf) | Yes | Standard embedding support |
| OpenType with CFF (.otf) | Varies | Some CFF-based OTF fonts cannot be embedded |
| PostScript Type 1 (.pfb) | No | Not supported at all |
| Variable fonts (.ttf) | Limited | Some variable fonts embed; others fail silently |

**Embedding permission levels** (set by font creator in the font file itself):
- **Installable**: Full embedding, no restrictions
- **Editable**: Can be embedded; recipient can edit the document
- **Preview/Print**: Can be embedded but recipient can only view/print
- **Non-embeddable**: Cannot be embedded at all

**Critical warning**: PowerPoint fails SILENTLY when a font cannot be embedded. No error, no warning -- the font data simply is not included. Always verify embedding after save.

**Google Fonts**: All Google Fonts use SIL Open Font License, which permits embedding without restriction. All 10 recommended pairings above are embeddable.

Source: [Microsoft Learn - Font Embedding](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/answers/questions/4991626/embedding-opentype-fonts-or-truetype-fonts), [Neuxpower Font Embedding Guide](https://support.neuxpower.com/hc/en-us/articles/360007786978-Embed-subset-or-remove-embedded-fonts-in-powerpoint-or-word), [Silver Fox Productions - Hazards of Embedding](https://www.silverfoxprod.com/blog/hazards-of-embedding-fonts-in-powerpoint-templates) [Confidence: HIGH]

### What Happens When a Font Is Missing

When a PPTX is opened on a machine without the specified font and the font is NOT embedded:

1. **PowerPoint performs automatic substitution**: It selects a "similar" font from the system's available fonts
2. **Text metrics change**: Character widths differ between the original and substitute font, causing text to overflow text boxes, wrap differently, or leave awkward gaps
3. **Visual design degrades**: The carefully selected typography is replaced with a generic fallback, often making the presentation look amateurish
4. **No warning on open** (usually): PowerPoint may show a brief notification about font substitution but does not block opening the file
5. **Fallback rules are NOT stored** in the PPTX: There is no way to specify "if Font X is missing, use Font Y" within the file format itself

Source: [BrightCarbon - Replace Fonts Workaround](https://www.brightcarbon.com/blog/replace-fonts-workaround-locating-pesky-powerpoint-fonts/), [Aspose - Fallback Font](https://docs.aspose.com/slides/net/fallback-font/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Font Subsetting with fonttools/pyftsubset

**Purpose**: Include only the characters actually used in the presentation, dramatically reducing font file size.

**Installation**: `pip install fonttools`

**Basic usage**:
```bash
pyftsubset input.ttf \
  --unicodes="U+0020-007E,U+00A0-00FF" \
  --layout-features="kern,liga" \
  --output-file="output-subset.ttf"
```

**Key parameters**:
- `--unicodes`: Specify Unicode ranges to include (U+0020-007E covers basic Latin)
- `--layout-features`: Keep kerning, ligatures, etc. (`"*"` keeps all features)
- `--flavor="woff2"`: Output as WOFF2 for web use (95% browser coverage)
- `--text-file`: Supply a file containing all characters used

**Real-world size reductions**:
- LiterataTT: 913KB -> 207KB (77% reduction)
- Typical TTF to WOFF2: ~70% size reduction

**Relevance to PPTX**: While pyftsubset is primarily a web font tool, subsetted fonts could be used with `pptx-embed-fonts` to minimize PPTX file size when embedding custom fonts. The workflow would be: subset the font to only characters used in the presentation, then embed the subsetted font.

Source: [fonttools Documentation - Subset](https://fonttools.readthedocs.io/en/stable/subset/), [Barrd.dev - Variable Font Subset](https://barrd.dev/article/create-a-variable-font-subset-for-smaller-file-size/), [Markos Kon - Creating Font Subsets](https://markoskon.com/creating-font-subsets/) [Confidence: HIGH]

Fonts that are guaranteed (or near-guaranteed) available by platform:

| Platform | Default Sans-Serif | Default Serif | Default Monospace | Notes |

| **Android** | Roboto | Noto Serif | Roboto Mono | Google-controlled ecosystem |

**Cross-platform safe fonts** (available on Windows + macOS + most Linux):
- Arial, Helvetica, Times New Roman, Georgia, Verdana, Trebuchet MS, Courier New

**The modern system font stack** approach:
```css
font-family: -apple-system, system-ui, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", Roboto, "Helvetica Neue", Arial, sans-serif;
```

**Key insight for PPTX**: Neither macOS, iOS, Chrome OS, nor Windows need you to specify the exact system font name. The operating system will use its default for the generic category. However, PPTX files store specific font names, so you MUST specify exact names.

### Practical Embedding Strategy for Jack-Tar Deckhand

Given the technical limitations, the recommended approach is:

1. **Default to universally available fonts**: Use Arial/Calibri as the PPTX font face names -- they are available on virtually all machines that run PowerPoint
2. **Design with Google Fonts aesthetics**: Use the pairing recommendations above for DESIGN guidance, then map to the closest universally available font
3. **Provide a "premium" path**: If the user has installed the Google Fonts, use those exact font names; otherwise fall back to safe equivalents
4. **Font installation helper**: Offer to download and install the required Google Fonts on the user's machine before generating the presentation

## Area 4: "Looks Good on Screen" vs "Looks Good Projected"

### Weight: The #1 Projection Killer

**Problem**: Thin and Light font weights (100-300) look elegant on retina/HiDPI screens but become illegible on projection. When extremely thin type is small, the strokes disappear against the background.

**Research finding**: People with low vision conditions have difficulty reading text set with font-weight 100 (Thin/Hairline) or 200 (Extra Light), especially at low contrast ratios. On projection, EVERYONE effectively has "low vision" due to distance and resolution degradation.

**Minimum weight recommendations for projection**:

| Context | Minimum Weight | Recommended Weight |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Heading text (36pt+) | Regular (400) | SemiBold (600) or Bold (700) |
| Body text (24-32pt) | Regular (400) | Medium (500) |
| Caption text (16-20pt) | Medium (500) | SemiBold (600) |
| Any text on image background | SemiBold (600) | Bold (700) |

**Rule of thumb**: The thinner the weight, the larger it must be. Thin/Hairline (100) should only appear at very large display sizes (72pt+) and never on projection.

Source: [Penn State Accessibility - Beware Light Weight Fonts](https://accessibility.psu.edu/legibility/lightweight/), [Pimp My Type - Avoid Light Fonts](https://pimpmytype.com/light-fonts/), [TypeEd - Font Weight Size](https://type-ed.com/resources/rag-right/2017/11/13/font-weight-size), [Fontfabric - Weight Typography](https://www.fontfabric.com/blog/typography-knowledge-weight-typography/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Stroke Contrast: Thin-Thick Variation

**Problem**: Fonts with high stroke contrast (dramatic variation between thick and thin strokes) lose their thin strokes on projection. The hairlines that look refined on a retina display become invisible at distance.

**Affected fonts from our pairings**: Playfair Display (HIGH contrast -- use with extreme caution on projection), Source Serif Pro (MODERATE contrast -- acceptable at larger sizes)

**Safe fonts**: Inter, Roboto, Montserrat, Open Sans, Poppins, Lato, Nunito -- all have LOW stroke contrast and maintain legibility across projection conditions.

**Research**: Low-contrast and non-contrast fonts are generally the most legible and readable. High-contrast typefaces can suffer from poor legibility, especially at small sizes, as thin strokes may disappear or break up. The smaller the text, the lower the contrast should be.

Source: [Fontfabric - Contrast Typography](https://www.fontfabric.com/blog/typography-knowledge-contrast-typography/), [ScienceDirect - Stroke Contrast and Low Vision](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0003687021001460), [ScienceDirect - Character Stroke Width](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0042698921000614) [Confidence: HIGH]

### X-Height: The Secret Weapon

**Principle**: Fonts with larger x-heights (the height of lowercase letters relative to uppercase) appear larger at the same point size and are more legible at distance.

**X-height comparison** (relative, larger = more projection-friendly):

| Font | X-Height | Projection Suitability |
|------|----------|----------------------|
| Inter | Very tall | Excellent |
| Roboto | Tall (4.68mm measured) | Excellent |
| Open Sans | Tall | Excellent |
| Montserrat | Large | Very good |
| Poppins | Tall | Very good |
| Merriweather | Very large | Excellent (for a serif) |
| Lato | Medium | Good |
| Playfair Display | Medium | Adequate (headings only) |

**Research finding**: Roboto's 4.68mm x-height improves mobile reading speed by 32%. This principle extends to projection: larger x-height = better distance legibility.

Source: [Fontdiff Inter vs Roboto](https://fontdiff.com/compare/inter-vs-roboto/), [Etienne Aubert Bonn - Inter Font](https://www.etienneaubertbonn.com/inter-font/), [Wikipedia X-Height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-height) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Character Width: Condensed Fonts Hurt Projection

**Research finding**: Character width is a more significant factor in legibility than serifs or stroke contrast. Condensed sans-serif fonts perform relatively poorly in legibility studies. Regular-width typefaces outperform condensed widths at distance.

**Implications for our pairings**:
- **Oswald** (condensed): Use ONLY for short headings at large sizes. Never for body text on projection.
- **Space Grotesk** (standard width): Fine for projection.
- **All other recommended fonts**: Standard or slightly wider widths, all acceptable.

**Guideline**: Avoid condensed or narrow fonts for any text that audiences need to read from beyond the first few rows. If space is limited, reduce word count rather than switching to a condensed font.

Source: [NNGroup - Typography for Glanceable Reading](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/glanceable-fonts/), [Extron - Font Size and Legibility](https://www.extron.com/article/videowallfontsize), [ResearchGate - Designing Legible Fonts for Distance](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/309747272_Designing_legible_fonts_for_distance_reading) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Serif vs Sans-Serif for Projection

**Research consensus**: There is NO definitive proof that sans-serif is more legible than serif on projection. A widely cited 2002 meta-analysis reviewed 50+ studies and concluded the research is "far too inconclusive to make any definitive claims."

**Practical consensus**: Sans-serif dominates presentation design because:
- Simpler letterforms are more legible at low resolution (projectors)
- Fewer fine details to be lost in projection
- Modern aesthetic aligns with tech/business presentation expectations
- Serif fonts with high stroke contrast (Playfair, Didot) genuinely struggle on projection

**Recommendation**: Default to sans-serif for body text. Use serif fonts (Source Serif Pro, Merriweather) for headings where they are large enough that stroke contrast is not an issue. Merriweather is specifically designed for screen reading and performs well on projection.

Source: [Springer - Legibility of Serif and Sans Serif](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-030-90984-0), [PMC - Serifs and Font Legibility](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4612630/), [Etienne Aubert Bonn - Serif vs Sans Serif](https://www.etienneaubertbonn.com/serif-vs-sans-serif/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Light Text on Dark vs Dark Text on Light

**Research summary**:

| Factor | Light Background + Dark Text | Dark Background + Light Text |
|--------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| General readability | 26% more accurate in studies | Lower accuracy overall |
| Bright room / ambient light | **Best choice** -- overcomes ambient light | Poor -- dark areas wash out |
| Dark room / dimmed venue | Good but may cause glare | **Best choice** -- reduces glare |
| Astigmatism (~50% of population) | Easier to read | Harder -- iris dilation causes fuzzy focus |
| Eye fatigue | Higher contrast can cause faster fatigue | Lower overall luminance reduces fatigue |
| Text weight adjustment | Use normal weights | Increase weight slightly; white-on-dark appears thinner |

**Edward Tufte's guidance**: Do not depend on verbal rules; test designs in actual presentation conditions. Reduce video glare by lowering figure-ground contrast. Pure white backgrounds create problematic screen glare; use light tints instead.

**Practical recommendation for Jack-Tar Deckhand**:
- **Default**: Light background (off-white, not pure #FFFFFF) with dark text (#1a1a1a, not pure #000000)
- **Dark mode option**: Dark background (#1a1a2e or similar) with light text (#f0f0f0, not pure #FFFFFF)
- **For dark mode**: Increase body font weight by one step (Regular -> Medium, or Medium -> SemiBold)
- **Always test**: Encourage users to preview on the actual projection system

Source: [Edward Tufte - Recommended Background](https://www.edwardtufte.com/notebook/recommended-background-for-projected-presentations/), [PolicyViz - Light vs Dark Backgrounds](https://policyviz.com/2014/04/03/light-vs-dark-backgrounds/), [NNGroup - Dark Mode](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/dark-mode/), [456 Berea Street - Light Text Readability](https://www.456bereastreet.com/archive/200608/light_text_on_dark_background_vs_readability/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Pango + Cairo: The Professional Choice

**What it is**: Cairo is a 2D vector graphics library; Pango is a text layout and rendering engine. Together (via PangoCairo), they provide professional-quality text rendering with full anti-aliasing, Unicode support (147+ languages), and advanced typographic features.

**Python usage**:
```python
import cairo
import gi
gi.require_version('Pango', '1.0')
gi.require_version('PangoCairo', '1.0')
from gi.repository import Pango, PangoCairo

# Create surface and context
surface = cairo.ImageSurface(cairo.FORMAT_ARGB32, width, height)
ctx = cairo.Context(surface)

# Create Pango layout
layout = PangoCairo.create_layout(ctx)
font_desc = Pango.FontDescription("Inter Bold 48")
layout.set_font_description(font_desc)
layout.set_text("Conference Title", -1)

# Render
PangoCairo.show_layout(ctx, layout)
surface.write_to_png("output.png")
```

**Advantages**: Built-in anti-aliasing, text wrapping, alignment, bidi support, font fallback chain, kerning. Does not require a browser process.

**Disadvantages**: Requires PyGObject/GTK dependencies (can be complex to install on some platforms). Less intuitive than CSS for styling.

Source: [PangoCairo Documentation](https://docs.gtk.org/PangoCairo/pango_cairo.html), [GitHub Gist - PangoCairo Text](https://gist.github.com/dov/ae4b889acb3659ca58fba1dba103ad49), [Aperiodic - Cairo Pango Python](https://aperiodic.net/pip/archives/Geekery/cairo-pango-python/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### Playwright: The HTML/CSS Approach

**What it is**: Headless Chromium browser automation that can render HTML/CSS to pixel-perfect images.

**Workflow for slides**:
1. Create an HTML template with CSS styling (fonts, colors, layout)
2. Launch headless Chromium via Playwright
3. Set viewport to slide dimensions (e.g., 1920x1080)
4. Take a screenshot of the rendered page

**Advantages**: Full CSS typography including `@font-face`, flexbox/grid layout, CSS animations (for GIF output), web font loading. Designers can style text using familiar CSS.

**Disadvantages**: Requires Chromium browser binary (~100-200MB). Browser startup adds latency. Font rendering may differ between platforms due to different anti-aliasing engines.

**Font loading**: Install Noto Fonts or other Google Fonts on the system; Chromium will use them. For custom fonts, use `@font-face` in the HTML.

Source: [Playwright Screenshots Docs](https://playwright.dev/docs/screenshots), [ScreenshotOne - Playwright Rendering](https://screenshotone.com/blog/how-to-render-screenshots-with-playwright/), [ScreenshotsAPI - Render HTML](https://screenshotsapi.dev/blog/render-html-with-playwright) [Confidence: HIGH]

### When to Burn Text Into Images vs Use Text Boxes

| Approach | When to Use | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|----------|------------|------------|---------------|
| **Text burned into image** | Complex visual layouts, text with effects (gradients, outlines), text over complex image backgrounds where positioning is critical | Pixel-perfect rendering, no font dependency issues, guaranteed appearance | Not editable in PowerPoint, accessibility issues (screen readers cannot read image text), larger file sizes |

| **Overlay: text-free image + text box** | Text over photos/backgrounds where you want BOTH visual control and editability | Best of both worlds: image provides visual foundation, text box provides editability and accessibility | Requires precise positioning; text box may shift relative to image on different screen sizes |

### Anti-Aliasing and Sub-Pixel Rendering Considerations

**For image generation** (PNG/JPG output for slide backgrounds):
- **Always enable anti-aliasing**: Without it, text looks jagged ("staircased")
- **Do NOT use sub-pixel rendering for images**: Sub-pixel rendering exploits the RGB sub-pixel layout of LCD screens. When rendered into an image file, these sub-pixel hints create visible color fringing. Images must use grayscale anti-aliasing.
- **Render at 2x resolution and downscale**: For the sharpest text in generated images, render at double the target resolution and scale down. This provides effective 2x anti-aliasing.
- **Target resolution**: For 1920x1080 slides, render text overlays at 3840x2160 and downscale. For print-quality, render at 3x.

**Cairo**: Uses grayscale anti-aliasing by default for image surfaces, which is correct for image output.
**Playwright/Chromium**: May use sub-pixel rendering by default. Disable it with CSS: `-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased;` or use the `--font-render-hinting` flag.
**Pillow**: Limited built-in anti-aliasing. Workaround: render 3x larger and downscale with `Image.LANCZOS`.

Source: [Wikipedia - Subpixel Rendering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subpixel_rendering), [Smashing Magazine - Ails of Typographic Anti-Aliasing](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2009/11/the-ails-of-typographic-anti-aliasing/), [Modern Text Rendering Linux](https://mrandri19.github.io/2019/08/08/modern-text-rendering-linux-ep2.html) [Confidence: HIGH]

### 1. Core Knowledge Base

- **Font weight is the single most important factor for projection legibility**: Minimum Regular (400) for body text, SemiBold (600) for headings. Thin/Light weights are invisible on projection. [Source: Penn State Accessibility](https://accessibility.psu.edu/legibility/lightweight/) [Confidence: HIGH]

- **The 8H rule provides the mathematical foundation for font sizing**: Back row distance / 400 = minimum text height on screen in inches. Then convert to PowerPoint points using slide dimensions. [Source: Presentation Guild](https://presentationguild.org/how-big-big-enough-the-8h-rule-reveals-all/) [Confidence: HIGH]

- **Neither PptxGenJS nor python-pptx support font embedding**: Font names are stored as string references only. - **All Google Fonts are SIL OFL licensed and permit embedding**: If embedding is achieved through external tools, there are no licensing restrictions for any of the 10 recommended pairings. [Source: Google Fonts](https://fonts.google.com/) [Confidence: HIGH]

- **Sans-serif vs serif is less important than x-height, weight, and contrast**: Research shows no definitive legibility advantage for either category. Choose based on visual character and audience expectations, not legibility dogma. [Source: Springer - Legibility Research](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-030-90984-0) [Confidence: HIGH]

- **Condensed fonts should never be used for body text on projection**: Character width is a more significant legibility factor than serif vs sans-serif. Oswald (condensed) is appropriate for short headings only. [Source: NNGroup - Glanceable Fonts](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/glanceable-fonts/) [Confidence: HIGH]

- **Light backgrounds work better in bright rooms; dark backgrounds work better in dark rooms**: 26% accuracy improvement for dark-on-light in general. For dark mode, increase font weight by one step to compensate for perceived thinning. [Source: NNGroup - Dark Mode](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/dark-mode/) [Confidence: HIGH]

### 2. Decision Frameworks

- **When choosing a font pairing**: If the presentation is technical and data-heavy, use Inter (single family) for simplicity and consistency. If the presentation needs visual personality, use a pairing with contrasting structure (Montserrat + Open Sans for modern, Playfair + Source Sans for elegant). If the audience is non-technical, use rounded fonts (Poppins + Roboto, Nunito + Nunito Sans) for approachability. [Confidence: HIGH]

- **When deciding font sizes**: Default to 28pt body / 44pt heading for medium conference rooms. For unknown venues, use Guy Kawasaki's 30pt minimum as the safe baseline. For data-heavy slides that require more text, go no lower than 20pt and warn the presenter about room size limitations. Apply the 8H formula if venue dimensions are known. [Confidence: HIGH]

- **When choosing between text box and burned-in-image text**: Use PptxGenJS text boxes as the default (editable, accessible, searchable). Burn text into images ONLY for complex visual effects (gradients, curved text, masking). Use the overlay pattern (text-free image + text box) for title slides and section dividers to get both visual impact and editability. [Confidence: HIGH]

- **When deciding whether to embed fonts**: If the presentation will be viewed on machines you control (your own laptop), do not embed -- install the fonts. If the presentation will be distributed to others, either (a) use universally safe fonts (Arial, Calibri), or (b) provide font installation instructions, or (c) post-process with pptx-embed-fonts. The safest strategy is to design with Google Fonts but set the PPTX font name to the closest universal equivalent (Inter -> Arial). [Confidence: MEDIUM]

- **When choosing a text rendering engine for image elements**: Use PyCairo + Pango for server-side/CLI rendering without browser dependency. Use Playwright when the design is already expressed in HTML/CSS. Use Pillow only for simple watermarks or captions where quality is not critical. [Confidence: HIGH]

### 3. Anti-Patterns Catalog

- **Using Thin/Light weights on projection**: Hairline strokes disappear on projectors. Always use Regular (400) minimum; prefer Medium (500) or SemiBold (600) for body text. [Source: Penn State Accessibility](https://accessibility.psu.edu/legibility/lightweight/)

- **Using condensed fonts for body text**: Oswald, Roboto Condensed, and other narrow fonts save horizontal space but dramatically reduce distance legibility. Use them only for short headings at large sizes. [Source: NNGroup](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/glanceable-fonts/)

- **Assuming fonts will be available on playback machines**: PptxGenJS and python-pptx do NOT embed fonts. A presentation designed with Inter will display in Arial (or worse) on machines without Inter installed. Always have a fallback strategy. [Source: PptxGenJS Issue #176](https://github.com/gitbrent/PptxGenJS/issues/176)

- **Using pure white backgrounds or pure black text**: Pure white (#FFFFFF) creates glare on projection. Use off-white (#F5F5F5 or similar). Pure black (#000000) creates excessive contrast. Use near-black (#1a1a1a or #2d2d2d). [Source: Edward Tufte](https://www.edwardtufte.com/notebook/recommended-background-for-projected-presentations/)

- **Using Playfair Display for body text**: High stroke contrast (thick-thin variation) makes hairlines invisible at body text sizes on projection. Reserve for headings at 36pt+. [Source: Fontfabric Contrast Typography](https://www.fontfabric.com/blog/typography-knowledge-contrast-typography/)

- **Using sub-pixel rendering in generated images**: Sub-pixel rendering exploits LCD pixel layout and creates color fringing when embedded in images. Always use grayscale anti-aliasing for PNG/JPG output. [Source: Wikipedia - Subpixel Rendering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subpixel_rendering)

- **Using more than 3 font sizes per slide**: Creates visual chaos and breaks hierarchy. Limit to title, body, and caption/label sizes on any single slide. [Source: BrightCarbon](https://www.brightcarbon.com/blog/presentation-font-size/)

- **Putting more than 50 words on a slide**: Per Nancy Duarte, 50+ words = teleprompter, 75+ words = document. The audience should grasp the slide in 3 seconds. [Source: Duarte](https://www.duarte.com/blog/design-slides-for-virtual-presentations/)

### 4. Tool & Technology Map

**Font Libraries for PPTX Generation**:
- **PptxGenJS** (MIT, Node.js): Primary PPTX generation. Supports `fontFace` property but NO font embedding. Current: v3.12+
- **python-pptx** (MIT, Python): Alternative PPTX generation. NO font embedding. Current: v1.0.0
- **pptx-embed-fonts** (npm, v0.0.6): Post-processing tool to embed fonts into PPTX. Low maturity.
- **Aspose.Slides for Python** (Commercial): Full font embedding support. Licensing required.

**Font Subsetting**:
- **fonttools/pyftsubset** (MIT, Python): Industry-standard font subsetting. `pip install fonttools`. 77% size reduction achievable.

**Text Rendering Engines**:
- **PyCairo + Pango** (LGPL, Python): Professional text rendering for image generation. Requires PyGObject.
- **Playwright** (Apache 2.0, Node.js): Headless Chromium for HTML-to-image. Full CSS support. ~200MB binary.
- **Pillow** (MIT-like, Python): Basic text rendering. Limited anti-aliasing. Best for simple overlays.

**Font Installation**:
- **Homebrew** (macOS): `brew install --cask font-inter`. Handles registration automatically.
- **fontconfig/fc-cache** (Linux/macOS): `fc-cache -fv` to register manually installed fonts.
- **google-font-installer** (npm): Programmatic Google Fonts download and install.

### 5. Interaction Scripts

- **Trigger**: "Create a presentation"
  **Response pattern**: Check if Google Fonts are installed (via fc-list). If not, offer to install them. Default to Inter (single family) if no preference stated. Set body to 28pt Regular, headings to 44pt Bold. Use light background unless user specifies dark mode.
  **Key questions to ask first**: What is the venue size? What industry/audience? Light or dark background preference?

- **Trigger**: "The text looks different on the presenter's laptop"
  **Response pattern**: This is a font availability issue. Check which fonts the PPTX references (examine XML). Verify those fonts are installed on the playback machine. If not, either install the fonts or replace with safe fallbacks.
  **Key questions to ask first**: What operating system is the playback machine? Is Microsoft Office installed (for Calibri)?

- **Trigger**: "Make the text bigger / I can't read this from the back"
  **Response pattern**: Apply the 8H rule. Get the room dimensions and screen size. Calculate minimum text height. Convert to PowerPoint points. Increase all text sizes proportionally.
  **Key questions to ask first**: How large is the room? How far is the back row? What is the screen/projector size?

- **Trigger**: "Use this specific font for the presentation"
  **Response pattern**: Check if the font is available on the system. Check if it has sufficient weights for hierarchy (need at minimum Regular + Bold). Assess projection suitability (x-height, weight range, stroke contrast). If the font is unsuitable for projection, recommend it for headings only and pair with a projection-safe body font.
  **Key questions to ask first**: Is this font installed? Will the presentation be shared or only presented from your machine?

- **Trigger**: "Make a dark/light mode version"
  **Response pattern**: For dark mode, increase body font weight by one step (Regular -> Medium). Reduce background luminance (use #1a1a2e, not pure black). Use off-white text (#f0f0f0, not pure white). Adjust any colored text for WCAG AA contrast ratio (4.5:1 minimum).
  **Key questions to ask first**: Will the room be dimmed or bright? Is there a brand color palette to integrate?

## Cross-References

- **Inter appears across all areas**: Recommended as the #1 default font (Area 1), has optimal x-height for projection (Area 4), is available via Homebrew (Area 6), and maps reasonably to Arial as fallback (Area 3). This convergence strongly supports Inter as the default font for Jack-Tar Deckhand.

- **Font weight recommendations in Area 4 directly constrain pairing choices in Area 1**: Raleway (pairing #5) and Playfair Display (pairing #2) require weight discipline on projection. The quick selection guide in Area 1 should include weight warnings from Area 4.

- **Font embedding limitations (Area 3) make font installation (Area 6) critical**: Since neither PptxGenJS nor python-pptx embed fonts, the only reliable strategy is ensuring fonts are installed. The font installation helper in Area 6 becomes a prerequisite, not an optional feature.

- **The overlay approach (Area 5) resolves the text-over-image dilemma from Area 4**: Using text-free background images with positioned PptxGenJS text boxes preserves both visual design AND font flexibility. If the font is missing on playback, the text box degrades gracefully to a fallback font while the background image remains pixel-perfect.

- **Font subsetting (Area 3) and container fonts (Area 6) share the same toolchain**: fonttools/pyftsubset can subset fonts for both embedding (Area 3) and container deployment (Area 6), reducing both PPTX file size and Docker image size.

- **Dark mode typography (Area 4) requires different weight settings than light mode**: This means the font pairing configuration (Area 1) needs to include weight variants per theme. Example: Inter Regular (400) for light body -> Inter Medium (500) for dark body.
