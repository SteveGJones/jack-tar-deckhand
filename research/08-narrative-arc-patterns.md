# Narrative Arc & Conference Storytelling Patterns

Research synthesis for the **narrative-architect** skill in Jack-Tar Deckhand. Covers conference talk structures, slide density, invisible slides, conference-specific patterns, slide type sequencing rules, and speaker notes intelligence.

## 1. Catalogue of Conference Talk Structures

### 1.1 Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR / Minto Pyramid)

**Origin:** Barbara Minto, McKinsey & Company, 1970s. Documented in *The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking*.

**Structure (3 beats):**

| Beat | Purpose | Content |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Situation** | Frame the context | Recent facts the audience already knows and accepts |
| **Complication** | Create urgency | What has changed; why action is needed now |
| **Resolution** | Propose the action | What must be done, how, by whom, by when |

**Extended form (SCQA):** Situation - Complication - Question - Answer. The Question makes the strategic question explicit before answering it.

**Ordering variants:**
- **S-C-R:** Use when building momentum; audience needs persuading
- **R-S-C:** Use when audience will generally accept your resolution but needs reinforced conviction (lead with the answer)

**Best suited for:** Executive briefings, strategy presentations, consulting decks, board updates.

**Slide count guidance:** 10-20 slides for a 30-minute talk. Spend 70% of time on Situation and Complication; 30% on Resolution.

**Example outline (30-min strategy presentation):**

| # | Slide Type | Beat | Content |
|---|-----------|------|---------|
| 1 | Title | -- | Talk title + speaker |
| 2 | Hook | Situation | Compelling statistic or story |
| 3 | Context | Situation | Market landscape / current state |
| 4 | Context | Situation | What we know and agree on |
| 5 | Tension | Complication | What has changed |
| 6 | Data | Complication | Evidence of the problem |
| 7 | Impact | Complication | Consequences if unaddressed |
| 8 | Section divider | -- | "So what do we do?" |
| 9 | Recommendation | Resolution | Core proposal |
| 10 | How it works | Resolution | Implementation plan |
| 11 | Timeline | Resolution | Milestones and owners |
| 12 | Evidence | Resolution | Proof / case study |
| 13 | Summary | Resolution | Key takeaways |
| 14 | CTA | Resolution | Specific next step |

**Sources:**
- [Speaking Sherpa: McKinsey SCR Framework](https://speakingsherpa.com/how-to-tell-a-business-story-using-the-mckinsey-situation-complication-resolution-scr-framework/)
- [Lenny's Newsletter: Minto Pyramid & SCR](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/minto-pyramid-principle-scr)
- [ModelThinkers: Minto Pyramid & SCQA](https://modelthinkers.com/mental-model/minto-pyramid-scqa)

### 1.2 Monroe's Motivated Sequence

**Origin:** Alan H. Monroe, Purdue University, 1930s. Based on study of influential speakers' techniques.

**Structure (5 beats):**

| Beat | Purpose | Typical Duration (30 min) |
|------|---------|--------------------------|
| **Attention** | Hook the audience | 2-3 min |
| **Need** | Establish a problem requiring change | 5-7 min |
| **Satisfaction** | Present the solution | 7-10 min |
| **Visualization** | Paint the future with/without the solution | 5-7 min |
| **Action** | Tell the audience exactly what to do NOW | 2-3 min |

**Best suited for:** Persuasive talks, fundraising, advocacy, calls to action, change proposals.

**Slide count guidance:** 15-25 slides for a 30-minute talk.

**Example outline (30-min persuasion talk):**

| # | Slide Type | Beat | Content |
|---|-----------|------|---------|
| 1 | Title | -- | Talk title |
| 2 | Hook image | Attention | Striking visual or question |
| 3 | Story | Attention | Compelling anecdote |
| 4 | Section divider | -- | "Here's the problem" |
| 5 | Data | Need | Evidence of the problem |
| 6 | Impact | Need | Who is affected and how |
| 7 | Quote | Need | Testimonial or expert voice |
| 8 | Section divider | -- | "There is a better way" |
| 9 | Concept | Satisfaction | The solution overview |
| 10 | How it works | Satisfaction | Step-by-step explanation |
| 11 | Evidence | Satisfaction | Proof it works (data, case study) |
| 12 | Section divider | -- | "Imagine if..." |
| 13 | Vision (positive) | Visualization | The world with the solution |
| 14 | Vision (negative) | Visualization | The world without it |
| 15 | Contrast | Visualization | Side-by-side comparison |
| 16 | CTA | Action | Specific, immediate action step |
| 17 | Resources | Action | How to get started |

**Sources:**
- [Lumen Learning: Monroe's Motivated Sequence](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/wm-publicspeaking/chapter/monroes-motivated-sequence/)
- [Grand Valley State University Speech Lab](https://www.gvsu.edu/speechlab/monroes-motivated-sequence-46.htm)

### 1.3 Hero's Journey for Tech Talks

**Origin:** Joseph Campbell, *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949). Adapted for presentations by Nancy Duarte and others.

**Structure (6 beats, simplified for presentations):**

| Beat | Purpose | Presentation Mapping |
|------|---------|---------------------|
| **The Ordinary World** | Establish the familiar | Current state, status quo, "the way things are" |
| **The Challenge** | Call to adventure | The problem discovered, the need for change |
| **The Journey** | Tests and trials | Approaches tried, lessons learned, obstacles |
| **The Transformation** | The breakthrough | The solution, the key insight, the turning point |
| **The New World** | Return with the elixir | Results, impact, the improved state |
| **The Call to Action** | Share the gift | What the audience should do with this knowledge |

**Key principle:** The audience is the hero, not the presenter. The presenter is the guide.

**Best suited for:** Conference keynotes, company all-hands, inspirational talks, transformation stories, case studies.

**Slide count guidance:** 20-35 slides for a 30-minute talk. Story-driven with image-heavy slides.

**Example outline (30-min keynote):**

| # | Slide Type | Beat | Content |
|---|-----------|------|---------|
| 1 | Title | -- | Talk title |
| 2 | Story image | Ordinary World | "Last year, our team was..." |
| 3 | Context | Ordinary World | The comfortable status quo |
| 4 | Tension | Challenge | The moment everything changed |
| 5 | Data | Challenge | Evidence of the problem |
| 6 | Quote | Challenge | "We knew we had to act" |
| 7 | Section divider | -- | "The Journey" |
| 8 | Story | Journey | First attempt (failed) |
| 9 | Story | Journey | Second attempt (partial success) |
| 10 | Lesson | Journey | What we learned |
| 11 | Section divider | -- | "The Breakthrough" |
| 12 | Concept | Transformation | The key insight |
| 13 | Demo/Evidence | Transformation | Proof it works |
| 14 | Data | New World | The results |
| 15 | Impact | New World | What changed for users/customers |
| 16 | CTA | Call to Action | "Here's how you can do this too" |

**Sources:**
- [Ethos3: Hero's Journey for Presentations](https://ethos3.com/why-you-need-a-heros-journey-for-your-next-presentation/)
- [The Good Deck: Hero's Journey as Design Tool](https://thegooddeck.com/blogs/presentation-designers-toolkit/the-heros-journey)
- [Duarte: Audience is the Hero](https://www.duarte.com/blog/presentation-storytelling-audience-is-hero/)

### 1.4 Problem-Solution-Impact

**Origin:** Classic pitch structure used widely in sales, startup, and business contexts.

**Structure (3 beats):**

| Beat | Purpose | Time Allocation |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Problem** | Make the audience feel the pain | 30-40% |
| **Solution** | Present how to fix it | 30-40% |
| **Impact** | Show the tangible outcomes | 20-30% |

**Key rule:** Stick to one primary problem per presentation. Focusing on multiple problems dilutes attention.

**Best suited for:** Sales pitches, client proposals, investor presentations, internal strategy decks.

**Slide count guidance:** 7-15 slides for a 20-minute pitch.

**Example outline (20-min pitch):**

| # | Slide Type | Beat | Content |
|---|-----------|------|---------|
| 1 | Title | -- | Product/company name |
| 2 | Hook | Problem | "Every day, X million people struggle with..." |
| 3 | Data | Problem | The cost of the problem |
| 4 | Story | Problem | Real user struggling |
| 5 | Section divider | -- | "There's a better way" |
| 6 | Concept | Solution | Product overview |
| 7 | How it works | Solution | Key features (3-5 max) |
| 8 | Demo/Screenshot | Solution | The product in action |
| 9 | Section divider | -- | "The results speak for themselves" |
| 10 | Data | Impact | Metrics and outcomes |
| 11 | Testimonial | Impact | Customer quote |
| 12 | CTA | Impact | Next steps / ask |

**Sources:**
- [Ink Narrates: Problem-Solution Presentation Structure](https://www.inknarrates.com/post/problem-solution-presentation-structure)
- [Winning Presentations: 7 Frameworks](https://winningpresentations.com/presentation-structure/)
- [First Round Review: Product Demos](https://review.firstround.com/Your-Product-Demos-Suck-Because-Theyre-Focused-on-Your-Product)

### 1.5 Problem-Demo-Impact

**Origin:** Product launch and developer talk pattern. Evolved from Silicon Valley demo culture.

**Structure (3 beats):**

| Beat | Purpose | Content Focus |
|------|---------|---------------|
| **Problem** | Frame the pain point | Market gap, user frustration, workflow friction |
| **Demo** | Show the solution live | Before > Action > After; think "show, don't tell" |
| **Impact** | Quantify the improvement | Time saved, errors reduced, users delighted |

**Key technique:** Follow a "Before > Action > After" flow in the demo. Keep the demo tightly scripted and tied to the problem stated earlier.

**Best suited for:** Product launches, developer conferences, technical showcases, startup pitches.

**Slide count guidance:** 10-15 slides + live demo segment. Demo replaces several slides.

**Example outline (25-min developer talk):**

| # | Slide Type | Beat | Content |
|---|-----------|------|---------|
| 1 | Title | -- | Product name + tagline |
| 2 | Story | Problem | Developer struggling with current approach |
| 3 | Data | Problem | Time/cost of the old way |
| 4 | Code snippet | Problem | The painful code |
| 5 | Section divider | -- | "Let me show you something" |
| 6 | Live demo | Demo | 5-8 minutes of live product demo |
| 7 | Architecture | Demo | How it works under the hood |
| 8 | Code snippet | Demo | The clean new code |
| 9 | Section divider | -- | "What this means for you" |
| 10 | Data | Impact | Performance improvements |
| 11 | Testimonial | Impact | Early adopter quote |
| 12 | CTA | Impact | "Try it today at..." |

**Sources:**
- [Claap: How to Demo a Product](https://www.claap.io/blog/how-to-demo-a-product)
- [daily.dev: Developer-Focused Demos](https://business.daily.dev/resources/create-developer-focused-demos/)
- [Zoho: Product Launch Presentation Guide](https://www.zoho.com/show/chronicles/step-by-step-guide-to-creating-a-product-lauch-presentation.html)

### 1.6 What-So What-Now What

**Origin:** Gary Rolfe (critical reflection in healthcare). Widely adopted in consulting, particularly McKinsey-style presentations.

**Structure (3 beats):**

| Beat | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **What** | Present the facts | "Revenue is up 12% but margin is down 3 points" |
| **So What** | Interpret the meaning | "We're winning more deals but at lower prices -- likely competitor pressure" |
| **Now What** | Recommend action | "I recommend we raise prices 5% on enterprise" |

**Key rule:** Apply this to every chart and data point. Data alone is meaningless; your audience needs to know what it means and what to do about it.

**Best suited for:** Quarterly reviews, analytics presentations, data-heavy reports, status updates.

**Slide count guidance:** 12-20 slides for a 30-minute data presentation. Each data point gets the full 3-beat treatment.

**Example outline (30-min quarterly review):**

| # | Slide Type | Beat | Content |
|---|-----------|------|---------|
| 1 | Title | -- | "Q3 Results: Why We're Accelerating Investment" |
| 2 | Executive summary | -- | Top 3 takeaways |
| 3 | Data chart | What | Revenue trend |
| 4 | Insight | So What | What the trend means |
| 5 | Recommendation | Now What | Proposed action |
| 6 | Data chart | What | Customer acquisition metrics |
| 7 | Insight | So What | What's driving the numbers |
| 8 | Recommendation | Now What | Where to invest |
| 9 | Data chart | What | Product usage data |
| 10 | Insight | So What | Feature adoption patterns |
| 11 | Recommendation | Now What | Product roadmap implications |
| 12 | Summary | -- | All recommendations consolidated |
| 13 | Next steps | -- | Specific actions + owners |

**Sources:**
- [Lewis C. Lin: What-So What-Now What Framework](https://www.lewis-lin.com/blog/what-so-what-now-what-framework-3-examples)
- [Liberating Structures: W3](https://www.liberatingstructures.com/9-what-so-what-now-what-w/)
- [StrategyU: Consulting Presentations](https://strategyu.co/consulting-presentations/)

### 1.7 Hook-Thesis-Body-Callback-CTA (Persuasion Talk)

**Origin:** Composite structure drawing from rhetorical tradition and the "bookend" technique used in public speaking.

**Structure (5 beats):**

| Beat | Purpose | Duration (30 min) |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| **Hook** | Grab attention in first 30 seconds | 1-2 min |
| **Thesis** | State the single idea you're arguing | 1-2 min |
| **Body** | Present evidence in 3 supporting points | 20-22 min |
| **Callback** | Reference the opening hook to create closure | 1-2 min |
| **CTA** | Tell the audience what to do | 2-3 min |

**Key technique (Bookend/Callback):** Reference the subject of your hook near the end of the talk. The first and last things the audience hears are the first things they remember.

**Best suited for:** Conference talks, advocacy, thought leadership, community talks.

**Slide count guidance:** 15-25 slides for a 30-minute talk.

**Example outline (30-min conference talk):**

| # | Slide Type | Beat | Content |
|---|-----------|------|---------|
| 1 | Image/story | Hook | Surprising anecdote or statistic |
| 2 | Thesis | Thesis | "Today I'll convince you that X" |
| 3 | Agenda (optional) | -- | Three supporting points preview |
| 4-7 | Evidence cluster | Body (Point 1) | Concept + data + example + takeaway |
| 8 | Section divider | -- | Transition to point 2 |
| 9-12 | Evidence cluster | Body (Point 2) | Concept + data + example + takeaway |
| 13 | Section divider | -- | Transition to point 3 |
| 14-17 | Evidence cluster | Body (Point 3) | Concept + data + example + takeaway |
| 18 | Callback | Callback | Return to the opening story/image |
| 19 | Summary | -- | The three points reinforced |
| 20 | CTA | CTA | Specific action + resources |

**Sources:**
- [Six Minutes: Bookending Your Speech](https://sixminutes.dlugan.com/bookending-speech-definition/)
- [I Hate Presentations: Bookend Your Presentation](https://www.ihatepresentations.com/bookend-your-presentation/)
- [Duarte: Persuasive Speech Communication](https://www.duarte.com/blog/art-of-persuasive-speech/)

### 1.8 Story-Point-Application (Rule of Three)

**Origin:** Classical rhetoric. The rule of three is a writing and communication principle found across cultures and centuries.

**Structure (3 x 3 beats = 9 total):**

| Cycle | Story | Point | Application |
|-------|-------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Personal anecdote or case study | The principle or insight | How the audience can apply it |
| 2 | Different story, same theme | Reinforces the pattern | Deepened application |
| 3 | Most powerful story | Delivers the payoff or twist | Final call to action |

**Key principle:** Three is the smallest number where humans recognize patterns. The first element sets up the expectation, the second reinforces it, the third delivers the payoff.

**Best suited for:** Motivational talks, leadership presentations, workshop keynotes, sermons.

**Slide count guidance:** 18-30 slides for a 30-minute talk (6-10 slides per cycle).

**Sources:**
- [Buckley School: Rule of 3 in Presentations](https://www.buckleyschool.com/magazine/articles/using-the-rule-of-3-to-elevate-your-presentations/)
- [Wikipedia: Rule of Three (writing)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_three_(writing))
- [Nathan Baugh: The Rule of Three](https://medium.com/@nathan.baugh/the-rule-of-three-ef617bd9b01b)

### 1.9 Pecha Kucha

**Origin:** Astrid Klein and Mark Dytham (Klein Dytham Architecture), Tokyo, February 2003. Created at SuperDeluxe event space in Roppongi to enable designers to "talk less, show more."

**Structure (fixed format):**
- 20 slides
- 20 seconds per slide (auto-advancing)
- Total: 6 minutes 40 seconds
- Slides must be predominantly image-based with minimal text
- Speaker cannot halt, return to, or skip slides

**Best suited for:** Design showcases, creative portfolios, community events, informal conferences.

**Slide count guidance:** Exactly 20 slides. No exceptions.

**Global reach:** As of 2019, PechaKucha Nights held in 1,142+ cities worldwide.

**Sources:**
- [PechaKucha Origins](https://pechakucha.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/360041374691-PechaKucha-Origins)
- [Klein Dytham Architecture](http://www.klein-dytham.com/pechakucha)
- [MasterClass: PechaKucha Explained](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/pechakucha-explained)

### 1.10 Ignite

**Origin:** Brady Forrest and Bre Pettis, O'Reilly Media, 2006. Inspired by Pecha Kucha but faster.

**Structure (fixed format):**
- 20 slides
- 15 seconds per slide (auto-advancing)
- Total: 5 minutes exactly
- Slides auto-advance with no speaker control

**Best suited for:** Tech meetups, community events, rapid-fire idea sharing.

**Slide count guidance:** Exactly 20 slides.

**Sources:**
- [Beautiful.ai: Lightning and Ignite Talks](https://www.beautiful.ai/blog/lightning-talks-and-ignite-talks-a-beginners-guide)

### 1.11 Lightning Talk

**Origin:** Conference community practice, widely used at PyCon, JSConf, and other developer conferences since the early 2000s.

**Structure (flexible):**
- 5-10 minutes (typically capped at 5)
- 10-15 slides (approximately 1 per minute, or more for visual-heavy talks)
- Multiple speakers present back-to-back
- Slides optional in some formats

**Best suited for:** Developer conferences, meetups, unconferences, rapid idea sharing.

**Key consideration:** At PyCon, 5-minute lightning talks are given by any attendee who wants the spotlight. The format values inspiration over instruction.

**Sources:**
- [Wikipedia: Lightning Talk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning_talk)
- [Alex Minney: Guide to Writing a Lightning Talk](https://alexminney.medium.com/guide-to-writing-a-lightning-talk-95fbd28bcc09)

### 1.12 The Lessig Method

**Origin:** Lawrence Lessig, Harvard Law professor and former presidential candidate. Popularized through his rapid-fire presentations on copyright and internet law.

**Structure:**
- Each slide contains just one word, phrase, quote, or picture
- Slides advance approximately every 15 seconds
- Text on slides is synchronized with the speaker's spoken words
- 200+ slides are common for a 60-minute talk
- Fast-paced, dynamic, and energetic

**Best suited for:** Advocacy talks, persuasive arguments, legal presentations, TED-style talks.

**Slide count guidance:** 2-4 slides per minute. A 30-minute talk may use 60-120 slides.

**Sources:**
- [Presentation Zen: The Lessig Method](https://presentationzen.blogs.com/presentationzen/2005/10/the_lessig_meth.html)
- [Ethos3: Presenting the Lessig Way](https://ethos3.com/designstyles-and-approachespresenting-lessig-way/)
- [Six Minutes: Lessig Method (Dick Hardt, Identity 2.0)](https://sixminutes.dlugan.com/presentation-20-hardt-executes-the-lessig-method/)

### 1.13 Takahashi Method

**Origin:** Masayoshi Takahashi, Japanese Ruby programmer, 2001. Created because he had no presentation software that handled his needs, so he used only giant text.

**Structure:**
- Only text slides (no charts, no images)
- One or two short words per slide in very large characters
- Many more slides than traditional presentations
- Each slide shown for a much shorter duration
- Emphasizes each point through huge, bold text

**Key difference from Lessig:** Takahashi emphasizes each point through large, bold text that "jumps out." Lessig emphasizes rapid pacing and dynamic energy. Takahashi is deliberate; Lessig is fast.

**Best suited for:** Technical talks, developer conferences, presentations without design resources.

**Sources:**
- [Wikipedia: Takahashi Method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Takahashi_method)
- [24slides: Alternative Presentation Styles - Takahashi](https://24slides.com/presentbetter/alternative-presentation-styles-takahashi)

### 1.14 The 10/20/30 Rule (Kawasaki)

**Origin:** Guy Kawasaki, venture capitalist and former Apple evangelist.

**Structure (fixed constraints):**
- **10 slides** maximum
- **20 minutes** maximum
- **30-point font** minimum

**Suggested 10-slide structure:**
1. Title
2. Problem / Opportunity
3. Value Proposition
4. Underlying Magic (technology)
5. Business Model
6. Go-to-Market Plan
7. Competitive Analysis
8. Management Team
9. Financial Projections / Milestones
10. Current Status + Ask

**Best suited for:** Investor pitches, startup presentations, high-stakes proposals.

**Sources:**
- [Guy Kawasaki: The 10/20/30 Rule](https://guykawasaki.com/the_102030_rule/)
- [Guy Kawasaki: Only 10 Slides in Your Pitch](https://guykawasaki.com/the-only-10-slides-you-need-in-your-pitch/)

### 1.15 Duarte Sparkline (What Is / What Could Be)

**Origin:** Nancy Duarte, *Resonate: Present Visual Stories that Transform Audiences* (2010).

**Structure (alternating contrast):**

```
What Is  ──┐    ┌── What Is  ──┐    ┌── What Is  ──┐
            │    │              │    │              │
            └────┘              └────┘              └──── New Bliss
       What Could Be      What Could Be      (Call to Action)
```

- Alternates between "what is" (current state, pain, challenge) and "what could be" (future vision, solution, benefit)
- Repeats the alternation multiple times throughout the presentation
- Builds toward a climactic "New Bliss" -- the call to action
- Maps changing emotions over the duration of the speech

**Key principle:** It cannot be just one transition from present to future. The "tacking" back and forth between what is and what could be is what persuades the audience to join the journey.

**Best suited for:** Keynotes, vision talks, company all-hands, transformation narratives.

**Validated by:** Analysis of Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" speech and Steve Jobs' iPhone launch.

**Sources:**
- [Nancy Duarte: The Secret Structure of Great Talks (TED)](https://www.ted.com/talks/nancy_duarte_the_secret_structure_of_great_talks)
- [Duarte: Ultimate Guide to Contrast](https://www.duarte.com/blog/ultimate-guide-to-contrast/)
- [Duarte: Resonate](https://www.duarte.com/resources/books/resonate/)

### 1.16 The Modular Deck

**Origin:** Consulting and sales practice for unpredictable meeting formats.

**Structure:**
- **Opening Module** (3-5 slides, always first)
- **Core Modules** (4-6 self-contained sections, reorderable)
- **Closing Module** (3-5 slides, always last)

**Key feature:** Sections can be rearranged or skipped based on audience needs. Uses PowerPoint Zoom for non-linear navigation.

**Best suited for:** Client meetings, consultations, unpredictable conversations, sales calls.

**Sources:**
- [Winning Presentations: 7 Frameworks](https://winningpresentations.com/presentation-structure/)

## 2. Slide Density Research

### 2.1 Evidence-Based Baselines

| Pace | Slides/Minute | When to Use |
|------|---------------|-------------|
| **Standard** | 1 slide per 1-2 min | Content-heavy talks, data presentations, technical deep dives |
| **Fast** | 1 slide per 30-60 sec | Visual-heavy talks, storytelling keynotes, Lessig-style |
| **Rapid-fire** | 2-4 slides per min | Pecha Kucha, Ignite, Lessig Method, Takahashi Method |

**Academic evidence:** "When you present your slide in the talk, it should take 1 minute or less to discuss. This rule is helpful for planning purposes -- a 20-minute presentation should have somewhere around 20 slides." (Heer & Bostock, PLOS Computational Biology, 2021)

### 2.2 Density by Talk Format

| Format | Duration | Typical Slide Count | Density |
|--------|----------|--------------------| --------|
| **Keynote** | 45-60 min | 40-60 (visual) or 20-30 (content) | Variable |
| **Breakout session** | 45-60 min | 30-50 | ~1 per min |
| **TED Talk** | 18 min | 10-20 | 0.5-1.1 per min |
| **Lightning talk** | 5 min | 10-15 | 2-3 per min |
| **Pecha Kucha** | 6:40 | 20 (fixed) | 3 per min |
| **Ignite** | 5 min | 20 (fixed) | 4 per min |
| **Investor pitch** | 20 min | 10 | 0.5 per min |
| **PyCon talk** | 30 min | 25-30 | ~1 per min |

### 2.3 Density by Speaker Style

| Style | Slides/Minute | Characteristics |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Storyteller** | 1-2 per min | Image-heavy, minimal text, cinematic pacing |
| **Technical deep-dive** | 0.5-1 per min | Code samples, architecture diagrams, data charts |
| **Lessig/Takahashi** | 2-4 per min | Single-word/image slides, rapid transitions |
| **Data presenter** | 0.5-0.75 per min | Complex charts, requires explanation time |
| **Demo-heavy** | Varies | Fewer slides; demo replaces slide content |

### 2.4 The Two Schools

**"60 slides for 60 minutes" school:**
- Visual-heavy presentations
- One simple idea per slide
- Rapid pacing keeps audience engaged
- Each slide is a visual support for the spoken word, not standalone content
- Best for: keynotes, storytelling talks, design presentations

**"20 slides for 60 minutes" school:**
- Content-heavy presentations
- Each slide warrants 2-3 minutes of discussion
- Audience needs time to absorb complex information
- Best for: technical deep dives, data presentations, board meetings, academic talks

**When to choose which:** Match slide density to content complexity. If your audience needs time to process each slide (data, architecture, code), use fewer slides. If slides are visual accents to your spoken narrative, use more.

### 2.5 Conference-Specific Guidance

**For a 45-minute conference talk:** "Talks typically use 25-125 slides per 45-minute presentation depending on speaking style." (Stephanie Walter, conference speaker)

- [PLOS: Ten Simple Rules for Effective Slides](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8638955/)
- [Stephanie Walter: Better Conference Slides](https://stephaniewalter.design/blog/create-better-conference-presentations-slides/)
- [Storyfiner: Perfect Slide Count](https://storyfiner.com/the-perfect-slide-count-how-many-slides-for-a-10-minute-presentation/)

## 3. "Invisible Slides" -- The Structural Elements

### 3.1 Section Dividers

**What they are:** Visual dividers that separate major chunks of a presentation, like chapter headings in a book.

**When to use:**
- Before every major topic shift
- At the transition between the three-act structure beats
- Before and after demo segments
- Every 8-12 slides in a long presentation

**What they contain:**
- Section title (concise, 3-6 words)
- Optional subtitle or guiding question
- Lots of negative space
- One clear focal point
- Distinct visual style from content slides (different background color or full-bleed image)

**Design principle:** Section break slides should be "the visual equivalent of taking a breath" -- minimal text, abundant negative space.

**Presenter technique:** When a section divider appears, pause. Let silence create weight. The pause signals that something is shifting. This is also the moment to physically move to a different stage position to reinforce the content shift.

**Sources:**
- [Ink Narrates: Section Break Slide](https://www.inknarrates.com/post/section-break-slide)
- [Winning Presentations: Presentation Pacing](https://winningpresentations.com/presentation-pacing/)

### 3.2 Breathing Room / Pause Slides

**What they are:** Blank, image-only, or minimal slides inserted for pacing. They provide "mental breathing room" for the audience to process what they've just heard.

**Types:**
- **Full-bleed image slide:** A compelling photograph with no text. Speaker narrates over it.
- **Blank/black slide:** Forces all attention to the speaker.
- **Quote slide:** A single quote related to the previous section. Gives the audience a moment to reflect.
- **Single-word slide:** A transitional concept word that bridges topics.

**Placement:** After data-heavy slides, after emotionally intense content, before major transitions.

**Sources:**
- [Presentation Team: Pacing and Pausing](https://www.presentationteam.com/tips-for-pacing-pausing/)
- [SlideModel: Power Pause](https://slidemodel.com/public-speaking-strategy-power-pause/)

### 3.3 Audience Interaction Beats

**What they are:** Slides designed to prompt audience engagement. They shift the presentation from monologue to dialogue.

**Types and timing:**
- **"Show of hands" slides:** "By a show of hands, how many of you have experienced X?" Best placed within the first 2 minutes and/or at the Need/Problem section.
- **Polling slides:** Use with tools like Polleverywhere.com. Display a question, collect responses via smartphones.
- **Reflection prompts:** "Take 10 seconds to think about a time this happened to you."
- **Discussion prompts:** For smaller sessions (workshops, chalk talks).
- **Scale rating:** "On a scale of 1-5, hold up your fingers..."

**Placement rules:**
- Within the first 2 minutes to establish connection
- Every 8-10 minutes to reset attention (the "10-minute rule")
- Before introducing a solution (to make the problem personal)
- Never during the climax or conclusion

**Sources:**
- [Duarte: Audience Engagement Strategies](https://www.duarte.com/blog/audience-engagement-strategies-presentations/)

### 3.4 Transition Cues / Bridge Slides

**What they are:** Slides that explicitly connect one topic to the next. They help the audience understand the shift.

**Verbal patterns:**
- "Now that we've covered X, let's move on to Y..."
- "Let's shift gears and discuss..."
- "Building on that..."
- "As we have seen... What this means is..."

**Visual patterns:**
- Progress indicators (step 2 of 5)
- Highlighted agenda (current section emphasized)
- Visual metaphor connecting previous and next topic

**Sources:**

### 3.5 Recap / Checkpoint Slides

**What they are:** Mid-talk summary slides that briefly recap what has been covered so far.

**When to use:**
- After completing a major section (every 10-15 minutes in a long talk)
- Before transitioning to a new topic
- Before a Q&A break
- At the midpoint of talks longer than 30 minutes

**What they contain:**
- 3-5 bullet points summarizing key takeaways so far
- Or a visual summary (timeline, checklist with checkmarks on completed topics)

**Phrasing:** "So far we have seen...", "To summarize this section...", "Let's take stock before moving on..."

**Benefit:** Attention typically drops midway through a presentation. A recap resets audience comprehension and ensures latecomers or distracted listeners can re-engage.

### 3.6 The Black Slide / Blank Slide Technique

**What it is:** Inserting a completely black (or blank) slide to redirect all audience attention from the screen to the speaker.

**How to activate:**
1. **Presentation remote:** Use the blackout button on wireless remotes (Kensington, Logitech R400)
2. **Keyboard shortcut:** Press "B" for black screen or "W" for white screen during slideshow mode
3. **Pre-inserted black slides:** Embed black slides at specific positions in the deck

**When to use:**
- During personal stories or emotional appeals
- When explaining complex ideas that require focused cognitive attention
- During audience Q&A
- When you want to redirect from visual to verbal communication

**Effects (from Florina Muntenescu's blank slides experiment):**
- Initial audience surprise (some suspected technical problems)
- After first blank slide, audiences accepted and focused on the speaker
- Reduced phone/email distractions
- Slides with content became more impactful through contrast
- Speaker gained better control over audience attention
- Required more thorough preparation and memorization

## 5. Slide Type Sequencing Rules

### 5.1 Core Sequencing Rules

**Rule 1: Never two data-heavy slides in a row.**
Follow any data/chart slide with an insight slide, story, image, or section divider. The 5/5/5 rule (no more than 5 text-heavy slides in a row) provides the outer boundary; the ideal is never more than 2 consecutive data slides.

**Rule 2: Section dividers before every major topic shift.**
Insert a section break slide before each new major topic. This gives the audience "mental breathing room and context for what's coming next."

**Rule 3: Progressive disclosure for complex ideas.**
Introduce concept > show data > draw conclusion. Break complex diagrams into smaller units across multiple slides, building up until the complete picture is revealed.

**Rule 4: One idea per slide.**
Each slide should have one central objective. If you spend more than 1 minute on a slide during practice, split it.

**Rule 5: Maximum 6 elements per slide.**
Keep the total number of visual elements (text blocks, images, chart elements) to 6 or fewer to avoid cognitive overload.

**Rule 6: Vary slide types rhythmically.**
Alternate between text, image, data, and interactive slides. Insert a change-of-pace element every 8-10 minutes to reset audience attention.

### 5.2 Opening Conventions

**Standard opening sequence (talks > 15 min):**
1. Title slide (on screen as audience settles)
2. Hook slide (surprising image, statistic, or question)
3. Thesis/agenda slide (for longer talks; skip for lightning talks)
4. First content slide

**Short talk opening (< 15 min):**
1. Title slide (brief)
2. Hook -- go directly into content
3. No agenda slide needed

**What NOT to do in the opening:**
- Don't start with a self-introduction (earn attention first)
- Don't start with "Can everyone hear me?"
- Don't use generic titles ("Q3 Financial Update" -- instead: "Q3 Results: Why We're Accelerating Investment")
- Don't show a table-of-contents slide with 10+ items

**The first 30 seconds rule:** Present your main idea within the first 30 seconds. Interact with the audience within the first 2 minutes.

### 5.3 Closing Conventions

**Standard closing sequence:**
1. Recap/summary slide (key takeaways)
2. Callback slide (reference the opening hook)
3. Call to action slide (specific next step)
4. Resources/contact slide (links, QR codes)
5. "Thank you" or Q&A slide

**What NOT to do in the closing:**
- Don't end with "Any questions?" as your final slide
- Don't introduce new content
- Don't rush the conclusion due to poor time management

### 5.4 The 10-Minute Attention Reset

Every 10 minutes, insert one of:
- An audience interaction beat (poll, show of hands, question)
- A story or anecdote
- A video clip
- A change-of-pace slide (different visual style)
- A recap/checkpoint slide
- A demo

**Science:** After 10 minutes, the brain begins to wander. Novelty, sensory variety, and emotional engagement help maintain attention.

**Sources:**
- [PLOS: Ten Simple Rules](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8638955/)
- [SlideGenius: 10-Minute Rule](https://www.slidegenius.com/blog/10-minute-rule-of-presentation-resetting-your-audiences-attention-every-ten-minutes)
- [Six Minutes: Sequence Your Presentation](https://sixminutes.dlugan.com/sequence-presentation/)

## 6. Speaker Notes Intelligence

### 6.1 Word Count Calibration

| Speaking Pace | Words/Minute | Use Case |
|---------------|-------------|----------|
| **Slow** | 100-120 WPM | Complex technical content, non-native audience |
| **Standard** | 130-150 WPM | General presentations, conference talks |
| **Fast** | 160-170 WPM | Motivational speeches, lively narratives |

**Per-slide word budget (at standard pace):**
- 1-minute slide: ~130-150 words in notes
- 2-minute slide: ~260-300 words in notes
- 30-second slide (visual accent): ~65-75 words in notes

**Key calibration:** If total speaker notes word count divided by speaking pace (WPM) exceeds the talk duration, content must be cut.

### 6.2 Timing Markers

Format timing markers as annotations in speaker notes:

```
[0:00] OPENING -- Title slide on screen as audience settles
[0:30] HOOK -- "Imagine you're sitting in a conference room..."
[3:00] SECTION 1 -- The Problem
[8:00] INTERACTION -- "Show of hands: how many of you have experienced this?"
[10:00] SECTION 2 -- The Solution
[18:00] CHECKPOINT -- Brief recap of sections 1-2
[20:00] SECTION 3 -- The Impact
[25:00] CALLBACK -- Return to opening story
[27:00] CTA -- "Here's what I want you to do this week..."
[28:00] Q&A
```

**Tip:** Mark short pauses after key points and charts. These create a natural buffer that makes timing easier to control.

### 6.3 Transition Cues

Include explicit transition cues in speaker notes:

```
TRANSITION: "Now that we've seen the problem, let me show you how we solved it."
CLICK: Animation builds on next click -- wait for chart to appear before speaking.
ADVANCE: Move to next slide after saying "...and that brings us to the key insight."
```

### 6.4 Audience Prompts

Embed audience interaction reminders in notes:

```
[AUDIENCE] Pause. Ask: "Raise your hand if you've seen this before."
[AUDIENCE] Wait 5 seconds for show of hands. Acknowledge the response.
[PAUSE] Let the audience absorb the data. Count to 3 silently.
[LAUGH] Pause for audience reaction after the joke.
```

### 6.5 Key Emphasis Markers

Mark words and phrases that need vocal emphasis:

```
The key insight is that *automation alone* is not enough.
We saw a **73% improvement** -- that's not incremental, that's transformational.
[SLOW DOWN] This is the most important point in the talk.
[LOUDER] "And that is why we must act now."
```

### 6.6 Speaker Notes Structure Template

For each slide, structure notes as:

```
[TIMING: ~2:00 | Minute 12-14 of 30]

MAIN POINT: [One sentence summary of the slide's key message]

TALK TRACK:
- Opening sentence for this slide
- Supporting detail 1
- Supporting detail 2 (if needed)
- Example or anecdote

TRANSITION: [How to move to the next slide]

STAGE DIRECTIONS:
- [CLICK] to advance animation
- [DEMO] switch to live demo window
- [PAUSE] let audience absorb
```

### 6.7 Notes Anti-Patterns

**Do NOT:**
- Write full scripts (you'll read them and lose audience connection)
- Use font sizes too small to glance at during presentation
- Skip transition cues (you'll stumble between slides)
- Forget timing markers (you'll run over)
- Include notes for "core moments" (opening, closing, key stories should be memorized)

**Do:**
- Use keywords and phrases, not full sentences
- Add keywords as you practice for points you tend to forget
- Glance at notes only at natural breaks (slide transitions, pauses)
- Keep your eyes on the audience at critical moments
